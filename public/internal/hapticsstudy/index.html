01 — Precedent
How existing capture devices handle it
No major handheld capture device has solved this cleanly. The iPhone comes closest — and even it shipped a documented failure that Apple had to patch at the OS level. That's a useful data point for where the industry is.
A
Apple iPhone Taptic Engine
Apple fires haptics on shutter tap, mode change, and recording start/stop while the camera is active. The critical insight: Apple built a dedicated OS-level API — setAllowHapticsAndSystemSoundsDuringRecording — specifically because the mic and haptic engine conflict by default. iOS disables standard haptic APIs when audio capture is active. Apple's native camera app bypasses this using a low-level system call (AudioServicesPlaySystemSound) that is completely independent of the audio session — it routes around the recording pipeline entirely.
Strategy: OS-level routing isolation
B
iPhone 17 Pro — active failure
In September 2025, iPhone 17 Pro shipped with a documented haptic-mic contamination issue. Users reported microphone hissing during video recording. The hissing was caused by the haptic motor's EM field coupling into the mic circuitry through shared chassis resonance. Apple confirmed a software fix. Root cause: the actuator and the mic array shared structural pathways through the metal body. Software isolation alone was not enough.
Failure: structural path coupling
C
DJI / GoPro action cams
DJI and GoPro do not implement haptics during recording at all. The actuator is electrically disabled the moment recording begins. Simplest solution, most common in the industry. It works, but it kills exactly the use case I'm building for: real-time confirmation while capture is happening, so the user knows the device is recording without looking at it.
Strategy: disable during capture
The Camera-specific constraint

Camera's haptic must fire confirmation pulses while recording. That's the whole point — the user isn't looking at the device. The haptic is the only confirmation channel. DJI's approach eliminates the use case. Apple's approach gets close, but their iPhone 17 Pro failure shows hardware isolation matters as much as software routing. I need both: signal-path decoupling and mechanical isolation between the actuator, the mic, and the lens system.

02 — Liquid lens risk
What haptic vibration does to a liquid lens
This was the question I was most uncertain about going in. The research literature answers it precisely — and the answer directly constrains which actuator I can use.
The mechanism Bessel mode surface waves External vibration induces axisymmetric standing waves on the liquid-liquid interface, degrading optical quality. Confirmed experimentally in peer-reviewed research on electrowetting lenses.
Lens resonance band 45–200 Hz Fundamental resonance for a 4mm aperture electrowetting lens. Directly overlaps with standard LRA and ERM haptic actuator operating frequencies — a direct conflict without isolation.
LRA haptic frequency 150–235 Hz Standard LRA resonant frequencies used in consumer devices. Falls within or adjacent to the liquid lens resonance band.
ERM motor frequency 100–300 Hz variable Sweeps through the entire resonance band continuously. Worst-case scenario for optical degradation.
The density-match fix Δρ ≤ 4 kg/m³ Lenses with density-matched fluids (Δρ = 4 kg/m³) are robust to external vibration. Lenses with Δρ = 250 kg/m³ show clear Bessel mode degradation. Corning Varioptic markets their lenses specifically on this vibration resistance.
Active suppression Voltage modulation Amplitude-modulated compensation applied to the lens drive voltage can reduce RMS intensity variation from 0.571 to 0.114. Backup option if density matching isn't confirmed sufficient.
Action item — internal
We have the Varioptic spec. Check the density delta (Δρ) of the fluid against the 4 kg/m³ threshold and confirm the resonance frequencies before finalizing the haptic spec. If density-matched, the architecture in section 06 holds as written. If not, mechanical isolation is added to the lens mount as a SOW line item.
03 — Audio contamination
How vibration enters the audio signal
Three distinct failure modes. Each has a different cause and a different fix. All three need to be addressed — they compound.
Failure mode A
Structural path coupling
Vibration travels through the chassis from actuator to mic capsule. Even if the mic element is electrically isolated, the chassis physically moves the diaphragm. 100–300 Hz is well inside mic sensitivity — it appears directly in the recording.
Fix: rubber isolation mounts, flexible PCB bridges, acoustic damping between actuator and mic mount points
Failure mode B
Electromagnetic coupling
LRA and ERM motors generate changing magnetic fields. If the MEMS mic element or its traces are within ~15mm of the actuator without shielding, the field induces current that appears as noise. This was the iPhone 17 Pro failure.
Fix: Faraday shield around mic circuit, ground plane between actuator and mic traces, minimum 20mm separation from any actuator
Failure mode C
Power rail contamination
The haptic driver IC draws pulsed current. If the mic analog front end shares a power rail with the haptic driver, switching noise appears directly in the audio chain. Pure PCB layout problem, independent of mechanical or EM issues.
Fix: dedicated LDO regulator for mic analog chain, fully decoupled from all actuator power rails

05 — The silent haptic answer
Answer to question 3: felt sensation without sound
Yes — and this was the most interesting finding. Two technologies achieve it. The physics explains why most haptics make noise and why these two don't.
Why LRA and ERM make sound
Any actuator that moves a physical mass through air generates audible pressure waves as a byproduct. The only way to eliminate audible sound is to eliminate mass movement entirely (thin-film piezo) or push the carrier frequency above 20 kHz (ultrasonic). There is no quiet ERM or LRA — the sound is a physics consequence, not an engineering failure.
Option 1 — thin-film piezo
How it works: The 150µm polymer film deforms the surface the finger is touching. Mechanoreceptors in the fingertip detect the deformation directly. No mass moves through air. No audible pressure wave is generated. Practically silent at typical actuation levels. Sensation quality: localized, textural, high-fidelity — can convey fine confirmation patterns at any frequency.
Option 2 — ultrasonic 40kHz
How it works: Phased array focuses acoustic pressure on the skin. 40 kHz is above human hearing. Modulation creates pressure variation that stimulates mechanoreceptors — felt, not heard. Zero sound in audible range. Constraint: transducer array currently too large for Camera's body at v1. Correct answer for v2.
Sensation character
Thin-film piezo delivers softer, more textural, localized sensation — less percussive than LRA. The design question this opens: should Camera's haptic language feel like a pulse (I captured something — discrete, countable) or a sensation (something moved through me — continuous)? Actuator spec resolves first. Then Maxime and I design the haptic language together from the available envelope, and hand it to firmware for implementation.
Answer to question 3

Yes — thin-film piezoelectric is the answer. It delivers felt sensation on the skin without producing audible sound, without shaking the device body, and without generating EM fields that contaminate mic circuits. It fits in 150µm. It's the right architecture for Camera.

06 — Architecture
How I'm thinking about building Camera's haptic system
Decision Spec Rationale
Actuator technology Thin-film piezo (KEMET or TDK PiezoHapt) Only option that fits the body, doesn't threaten the liquid lens, and is practically silent during recording.
Placement Grip zone only Minimum 25mm from liquid lens assembly and microphone capsule. Film bonds to inner surface of housing at thumb/finger contact zone.
Actuation frequency Above 250 Hz or below 40 Hz Avoids the 45–200 Hz electrowetting lens resonance band. 250–400 Hz provides clean confirmation pulses outside lens sensitivity.
Lens mount Floating elastomer mount Lens module suspended on compliant elastomer, mechanically decoupled from haptic actuation path through chassis wall.
Mic isolation Separate LDO + Faraday shield Mic analog chain on dedicated LDO, no shared power with haptic driver. Ground plane and Faraday shield around mic circuit. 20mm+ from any actuator element.
Signal routing Decoupled from audio session Haptic events fire through a path that never enters the audio processing pipeline — equivalent of Apple's AudioServicesPlaySystemSound at the firmware level.
Validation FFT during recording Spectrum analysis of recorded audio during all planned haptic event types. Target: less than 3dB above noise floor. Any energy in 20 Hz–20 kHz = failure.
Where I land

Committing to thin-film piezo as the actuator architecture. It's the only option that fits the body, doesn't threaten the liquid lens, is practically silent during recording, and is aligned with what Camera should feel like. That decision is made.

Maxime and I will design the haptic language together — working from the actuation envelope the spec'd actuator provides: available frequencies, pulse durations, intensity curves, and timing constraints. The output of that process is a defined haptic language document that goes to firmware and embedded for implementation. Interaction design owns the language. Engineering owns the implementation.

The only open technical item before closing the haptic spec is confirming the liquid lens density delta. We have the Varioptic spec — need to verify Δρ against the 4 kg/m³ threshold and confirm resonance frequencies. If density-matched, the architecture above holds.
